# Glossary of Terms

## A

**Actuator**: A component of a robot that converts energy (usually electrical) into physical motion. Examples include servomotors, stepper motors, and hydraulic cylinders.

**AI (Artificial Intelligence)**: The simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, especially computer systems. In robotics, AI enables perception, decision-making, and learning capabilities.

**Artificial Neural Network (ANN)**: A computing system inspired by biological neural networks that constitute animal brains. Such systems learn to perform tasks by considering examples.

## B

**Bipedal**: Having two feet or legs; refers to walking on two legs like humans.

**Bipedal Locomotion**: The act of walking on two legs, which is one of the most challenging problems in humanoid robotics.

**Body Schema**: A representation of the robot's body configuration, including joint angles and limb positions, used for movement planning and control.

## C

**Computer Vision**: A field of artificial intelligence that trains computers to interpret and understand the visual world. Using digital images from cameras and videos and deep learning models, machines can accurately identify and classify objects.

**Control Theory**: A field of engineering and mathematics that deals with the behavior of dynamical systems with inputs, and how their behavior is modified by feedback.

**Cognitive Architecture**: An overarching structure of the mind that determines how information is processed and guides behavior.

## D

**Deep Learning**: A subset of machine learning that uses neural networks with three or more layers. These neural networks attempt to simulate the behavior of the human brain.

**Degrees of Freedom (DOF)**: The number of independent movements a joint or mechanical system can make. In robotics, it refers to the number of independent parameters that define the configuration of a kinematic chain.

**Dynamics**: The study of forces and torques and their effect on motion. In robotics, it involves understanding how forces affect robot movement.

## E

**Embodied AI**: Artificial intelligence that is situated in a physical body and interacts with the real world, as opposed to AI that operates only in digital environments.

**Embodied Cognition**: The theory that many features of cognition, whether human or otherwise, are shaped by aspects of the entire body of the organism.

**End Effector**: The device at the end of a robotic arm, designed to interact with the environment. Examples include grippers, tools, and sensors.

## F

**Forward Kinematics**: The use of joint parameters to compute the Cartesian position and orientation of the end effector.

**Force Control**: A control strategy that regulates the forces applied by a robot to its environment, often used in manipulation tasks.

## G

**Gait**: The pattern of movement of the limbs of legged robots, particularly the relative timing of the limbs as they make contact with the ground.

**General Artificial Intelligence (AGI)**: A type of artificial intelligence that can understand, learn, and apply knowledge across a wide range of tasks at a level equal to human intelligence.

**Gripper**: A device used to grasp and hold objects, typically found at the end of a robot arm.

## H

**Humanoid Robot**: A robot with physical characteristics resembling those of a human, typically having a head, torso, two arms, and two legs.

**Human-Robot Interaction (HRI)**: The field of study dedicated to understanding, designing, and evaluating robotic systems for human interaction.

## I

**Inverse Kinematics**: The mathematical process of calculating the variable joint parameters needed to place the end of a kinematic chain in a given position and orientation.

**Imitation Learning**: A technique in machine learning where an agent learns to perform tasks by observing and mimicking expert demonstrations.

**Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU)**: An electronic device that measures and reports a body's specific force, angular rate, and sometimes the magnetic field surrounding the body.

## K

**Kinematics**: The branch of mechanics concerned with the motion of objects without reference to the forces that cause the motion.

**Kinematic Chain**: An assembly of rigid bodies connected by joints that is the mathematical model for a mechanical system.

## L

**Legged Locomotion**: The act of moving using legs, a challenging problem in robotics due to the need for balance and coordination.

**LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging)**: A remote sensing method that uses light in the form of a pulsed laser to measure distances.

**Locomotion**: The ability to move from one place to another, a fundamental capability for mobile robots.

## M

**Manipulation**: The capability of a robot to purposefully control objects in its environment using its end effectors.

**Machine Learning**: A method of data analysis that automates analytical model building. It is a branch of artificial intelligence based on the idea that systems can learn from data.

**Middleware**: Software that provides common services and capabilities to applications beyond what's offered by the operating system.

## N

**Navigation**: The ability of a robot to move through its environment safely and efficiently, often involving mapping and path planning.

**Neural Network**: A series of algorithms that endeavors to recognize underlying relationships in a set of data through a process that mimics the way the human brain operates.

## P

**Path Planning**: The computational problem of finding a sequence of valid configurations that moves an object from a source to a destination.

**Perception**: The ability of a robot to sense and interpret its environment using various sensors.

**PID Controller**: A control loop feedback mechanism widely used in industrial control systems that calculates an error value as the difference between a desired setpoint and a measured process variable.

## R

**Reinforcement Learning**: A type of machine learning where an agent learns to make decisions by performing actions and receiving rewards or penalties.

**Robot Operating System (ROS)**: A flexible framework for writing robot software. It is a collection of tools, libraries, and conventions that aim to simplify the task of creating complex and robust robot behavior.

**ROS 2**: The second generation of the Robot Operating System, designed to be suitable for production environments.

## S

**Sensor Fusion**: The process of combining data from multiple sensors to achieve better accuracy and reliability than could be achieved by using a single sensor.

**SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)**: The computational problem of constructing or updating a map of an unknown environment while simultaneously keeping track of an agent's location within it.

**Simulation**: The computational modeling of real-world processes to predict behavior under various conditions.

## T

**Torque**: A rotational force that can cause an object to rotate around an axis, important in controlling robot joint movement.

**Trajectory Planning**: The process of creating a path that a robot will follow, taking into account dynamics, kinematics, and environmental constraints.

## V

**Vision System**: A robot's ability to see and interpret visual information from cameras and other optical sensors.

**Voice Recognition**: The ability of a machine or program to receive and interpret dictation or to understand and carry out spoken commands.

## W

**Walking Pattern Generator**: An algorithm or system that produces the rhythmic patterns necessary for bipedal locomotion.

**Whole-Body Control**: A control approach that considers the entire robot as a system and optimizes all degrees of freedom simultaneously.
